# Content Quality

Load this when the audit passes the technical stages and the real question becomes *does this page
deserve to rank*. Source: Google's Search Quality Rater Guidelines (September 11, 2025 edition) and
the SEO Starter Guide. Check the source before asserting a current specific — see the "Sources"
section of `../SKILL.md`.

## What this framework is, and what it is not

Quality raters score pages so Google can measure whether its systems are working. Their ratings are
**not** a ranking mechanism. The guidelines state it directly:

> "No single rating can directly impact how a particular webpage, website, or result appears in
> Google Search, nor can it cause specific webpages, websites, or results to move up or down on the
> search results page."

So use this as a **diagnostic lens** — a structured way to ask whether a page is actually good — not
as a scoring rubric with a number to optimize. Any claim that a page "has an E-E-A-T score" or that
raters penalized a site is false. Say so if a user asserts it.

## Start with page purpose

Every page is created for a purpose, and quality means **how well the page achieves that purpose**.
You cannot judge a page without first stating what it is for.

- No page *type* outranks another by type. An encyclopedia page is not inherently better than a humor
  page; each is judged against its own purpose.
- A page created to benefit the owner with little or no attempt to benefit visitors is Lowest quality
  regardless of polish.
- A page with a harmful or deceptive purpose is Lowest, and no other quality signal rescues it.

The guidelines split a page into **Main Content (MC)** — the part that achieves the purpose —
**Supplementary Content (SC)**, and **Ads/Monetization**. When you audit content, identify the MC
first; that is what is being judged.

## YMYL: how much scrutiny the page needs

"Your Money or Your Life" topics are those where inaccurate content could significantly harm health,
financial stability, safety, or societal welfare. YMYL is a **spectrum**, not a flag: clear YMYL,
possibly YMYL, clearly not.

Assess harm in four areas: health/safety, financial security, government/civics/society, and other
societal welfare.

Practical consequence: on clear YMYL topics, demand accuracy, expert sourcing, and clear authorship.
On a page about music trivia or washing jeans, that same demand is over-engineering. Calibrate the
strictness of your content critique to the harm the topic can cause — do not apply medical-grade
scrutiny to a recipe blog.

## E-E-A-T, with Trust at the center

Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trust. **Trust is the most important member**; the
other three exist to support an assessment of it.

> "Untrustworthy pages have low E-E-A-T no matter how Experienced, Expert, or Authoritative they may
> seem."

- **Trust** — is the page accurate, honest, safe, and reliable? How much is needed depends on the
  page: an online store needs secure payment and real customer service; a social post meant to
  entertain needs very little.
- **Experience** — does the creator have first-hand experience of the thing? (A review by someone who
  owns the product beats a "review" by someone who does not.)
- **Expertise** — does the creator have the knowledge or skill for the topic?
- **Authoritativeness** — is the creator or site a known go-to source for this topic?

Assess it from three angles: what the site says about itself (About/contact pages), what independent
sources say about it (reputation, reviews, references), and what is visible in the MC itself.

Two traps worth naming to a user:

- **Conflict of interest degrades trust.** A manufacturer's "review" of its own product, or a paid
  influencer's, is not trustworthy content no matter how expert.
- **On YMYL topics, first-hand experience and formal expertise are not interchangeable.** Someone
  describing how they coped with an illness can be high quality; someone without medical training
  giving treatment advice is not. Ask which of the two the page is actually doing.

## The Lowest-quality screen

Run this before any nuanced quality judgment — it is a screen, not a score. **Any one** of these
justifies Lowest:

- **Harmful purpose or deceptive design** — including hiding who is responsible for the content.
- **Created only to benefit the owner**, with little or no attempt to help the visitor.
- **Harmful to self or others, harmful to groups, or harmfully misleading.**
- **MC created with little effort, little originality, and no added value** compared to similar pages
  on the web — including scaled content abuse, expired-domain abuse, and site-reputation abuse.
- **Extremely misleading, shocking, or exaggerated title.**
- **MC deliberately obstructed** by ads, interstitials, or supplementary content.
- **No information about who is responsible** for a YMYL page or any page requiring trust.
- **Very negative reputation**, or a hacked/defaced/spammed/gibberish page.

Note the guidelines' own caution: plenty of content is controversial, one-sided, or distasteful
without meeting Lowest. Judge against the criteria, not against personal taste.

## What good content actually means here

Working upward, High and Highest quality pages combine: MC that satisfies the purpose with real
effort, originality, and skill; a positive reputation; and a level of E-E-A-T appropriate to the
topic. That is the whole of it. The Starter Guide's own conclusion is worth quoting back to any user
chasing tricks:

> "Creating content that people find compelling and useful will likely influence your website's
> presence in search results more than any of the other suggestions in this guide."

## Needs Met: relevance is separate from quality

The raters run a second, independent scale — **Needs Met** — measuring how well a result satisfies
the *query* (Fully Meets → Highly Meets → Moderately Meets → Slightly Meets → Fails to Meet).

The distinction is the useful part: **a page can be high quality and still fail the query.** An
excellent article that does not match the searcher's intent is not a ranking candidate for that
search. When a user asks "why doesn't my great page rank," the answer is often intent mismatch, not
quality — check what the ranking pages for that query actually *do* (inform? sell? let the user do
something?) and whether the page does that at all.
